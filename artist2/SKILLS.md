# Student Skills

## Current Level: Intermediate-Advanced

### Tools Mastery
- **Pen**: Solid proficiency - can draw lines, circles, arcs, rectangles, and triangles with controlled pressure
- **Pencil**: Solid proficiency - can draw ellipses, S-curves, and lines with varied pressure and color
- **Brush**: Intermediate proficiency - can draw circles, ellipses, and gradient fills with expressive pressure variation
- **Charcoal**: Intermediate proficiency - practiced arcs and gradient fills; produces rough, textured marks distinct from pen and pencil; gradient fills have a distinctive gritty quality
- **Marker**: Intermediate proficiency - used for bold foreground texture (grass hatching, tree trunk, ground edge); produces high-contrast graphic marks ideal for near elements

### Techniques
- **Lines**: Practiced horizontal, vertical, and diagonal lines with precise placement
- **Curves**: Practiced S-curves with varying amplitude (15, 25, 30, 40); understand how amplitude controls the drama of the S shape
- **Circles & Arcs**: Can draw full circles, concentric circles, half-arcs, and quarter-arcs; understand radian angle system (0=right, pi/2=down, pi=left, 3pi/2=up, clockwise)
- **Ellipses**: Can draw ellipses with independent horizontal and vertical radii; wide (rx>ry) and tall (ry>rx) orientations
- **Shapes**: Can draw rectangles (including squares) and triangles (right triangles and isosceles)
- **Hatching**: Solid proficiency - can create parallel-line tone at varying spacings (5, 8, 12, 14) and angles (0, 30, 45, 75 degrees) to control density and direction; used for value scales and form shading
- **Crosshatching**: Solid proficiency - can layer hatching at multiple angles (default 45/-45) for richer, denser tone; understand that crosshatching produces darker values than single-direction hatching at same spacing
- **Gradient Fill**: Solid proficiency - can create smooth tonal transitions from light to dark (and reversed) using draw_gradient_fill; tested with pencil, charcoal, and brush tools
- **Pressure Control**: Practiced pressures from 0.2 (very light) to 0.9 (very heavy); understand how pressure affects line weight across all tools
- **Color Mixing**: Practiced - can overlay crosshatching in complementary colors (red/green, blue/orange) to create optical mixing effects; understand how spacing differences between layers affect the visual blend

### Artistic Concepts
- **Composition**: Solid proficiency - organized canvas into four-quadrant layout; practiced rule-of-thirds placement with focal point at power point; composed a multi-object still life with deliberate warm/cool contrast and spatial depth (foreground table, background wall, objects at varying depths)
- **Perspective**: Intermediate - practiced atmospheric perspective with three depth zones; understand how value, color temperature, detail density, and tool choice work together to create the illusion of distance
- **Light & Shadow**: Beginning to understand - practiced shading a sphere and cube with top-left light source; know that surfaces facing the light are lightest (low pressure, wide spacing) and surfaces turned away are darkest (high pressure, tight spacing); understand core shadow, cast shadow, and tonal transition zones
- **Color Theory**: Intermediate understanding - practiced warm vs cool color contrast (red/orange/yellow vs blue/green/purple); understand complementary color pairs (red/green, blue/orange, yellow/purple) and how they intensify each other; used warm colors for advancing focal points and cool colors for receding backgrounds; shaded colored objects using darker versions of the same hue (not black) to maintain color vibrancy
- **Texture**: Observed how different tools (pen, pencil, brush, charcoal) produce distinct visual textures; charcoal is notably rougher than pen
- **Expression**: Growing confidence - added decorative elements beyond strict requirements to create visual interest (dot-center in concentric circles, fading ripple curves, nested squares, mirrored arcs); in Lesson 5 made independent compositional decisions (dock, winding path, wildflower placement, bird silhouettes, cloud wisps)
- **Portraiture**: Beginning proficiency - can construct faces with correct proportions (oval head, eyes at midline, nose and mouth at proper positions); can draw eyes with iris, pupil, and highlight; eyebrows that convey emotion; understated noses; and expressive mouths; can shade faces with light/shadow side, under-nose shadow, jaw shadow, and eye socket shadows; can draw three distinct hair styles (wavy, straight long, curly short) using appropriate tools

### Notes
- Pen produces clean, sharp lines with consistent edges
- Pencil produces softer, slightly textured lines
- Brush produces broad strokes that respond dramatically to pressure changes
- Charcoal produces rough, gritty marks with an organic feel
- Pressure ramp creates a smooth thin-to-thick-to-thin transition
- Canvas coordinates: origin at top-left, Y increases downward (have used 800x600 and 900x700)
- Arc angles are in radians: full circle = 0 to 2*pi, half = 0 to pi or pi to 2*pi, quarter = 0 to pi/2
- S-curve amplitude controls how far the curve swings from the straight path between endpoints
- Concentric shapes (circles within circles, rectangles within rectangles) create a sense of depth
- Repeating a motif at decreasing intensity (pressure/size) creates rhythm and visual echo
- draw_hatching and draw_crosshatching return LISTS of strokes -- must use extend(), not append()
- draw_gradient_fill also returns a LIST -- must use extend()
- draw_crosshatching takes `angles` parameter (a list like [45, -45]), NOT `angle_deg`
- Spacing controls apparent darkness as much as pressure: spacing=5 at pressure=0.3 can look darker than spacing=14 at pressure=0.6
- Overlapping hatching zones at different densities create the illusion of rounded form (sphere shading)
- Different hatching angles on adjacent cube faces help the eye distinguish the surfaces
- Cast shadows ground an object and prevent it from appearing to float
- Value (lightness/darkness) is the primary tool for creating 3D illusion on a 2D surface
- draw_line takes separate x1, y1, x2, y2 arguments -- NOT tuples like (x1,y1), (x2,y2)
- draw_pressure_ramp also takes separate x1, y1, x2, y2 arguments
- Warm colors (red, orange, yellow, brown, pink) advance toward the viewer; cool colors (blue, green, purple, cyan) recede
- Complementary pairs: red/green, blue/orange, yellow/purple -- placing complements side by side intensifies both
- Rule of thirds: on a 900x700 canvas, power points are at approximately (300,233), (600,233), (300,467), (600,467)
- Focal point tools: contrast, detail, isolation, and warm color amid cool surroundings
- Shade colored objects with darker versions of the same hue (e.g., dark red for apple shadow) -- black hatching over color looks muddy
- RGB tuples like (180, 150, 110) work as color parameters alongside named colors
- Layer order matters: draw backgrounds first, then objects, then outlines and shadows on top
- Yellow is inherently light-valued, red is mid-valued, blue is dark-valued -- adjust hatching density accordingly
- draw_filled_circle returns a LIST -- must use extend()
- Atmospheric perspective has four channels: value (light to dark), color temperature (cool to warm), detail density (sparse to dense), and tool softness (brush/charcoal for distance, pen/marker for near)
- Build landscapes back to front: sky, distant hills, middle ground, foreground, details -- each layer overlaps and covers the previous
- Natural elements are composed from primitives: a tree is lines (trunk/branches) + filled circles (canopy) + hatching (texture); a lake is a filled rectangle + horizontal lines (ripples)
- Overlap is the most powerful depth cue -- a hill overlapping the sky reads as in front of the sky
- Charcoal at pressure 0.1-0.2 produces a soft atmospheric haze perfect for distant hills
- Marker at pressure 0.4-0.6 produces bold graphic marks ideal for foreground texture and tree trunks
- draw_curve takes a list of (x,y) tuples as control points and produces smooth Catmull-Rom spline curves -- useful for ridgelines and organic shapes
- Multiple overlapping filled circles at slightly different positions create convincing organic masses (tree canopy)
- A "golden hour" mood is achieved with deep blue sky top, warm amber mid-sky, gold horizon, and a sun as a filled circle with a faint glow circle behind it
- Leading lines (paths, docks, shorelines) guide the viewer's eye through the composition
- Small creative details (birds, flowers, dock) add life and personality to a scene without overwhelming the composition
- Canvas 1000x600 works well for panoramic landscape compositions
- Face proportions: eyes at vertical midline of head oval, nose halfway between eyes and chin, mouth 1/3 from nose to chin, ears aligned top-at-eyebrow bottom-at-nose
- Expression is carried by three elements: mouth curve direction (up=happy, down=sad, flat=neutral), eyebrow angle (inner-up=worried/sad, arched=happy, level=calm), and eye openness (squinted=happy, droopy=sad, steady=thoughtful)
- Eye highlights (tiny white filled circles at 1-2px radius) make eyes look alive -- without them eyes look flat and dead
- Understated noses (short bridge line + small tip arc + tiny nostril arcs) read better than fully modeled noses
- Build portraits back-to-front: neck/shoulders, skin fill, shading, head outline, facial features, hair on top
- Hair flows FROM a crown/parting point -- all strands radiate outward and downward from there
- Wavy hair: S-curves from crown with varied amplitude; straight hair: long lines from part; curly hair: overlapping small circles
- Use charcoal for hair mass/texture (rough organic quality) and pencil for highlight strands (softer, lighter)
- Marker works well for bold eyebrows on expressive faces (happy, angry) while pencil suits calm/contemplative expressions
- A parameterized portrait function (expression, hair_style, eye_color, hair_color) enables consistent proportions across multiple studies while varying the emotional content
- Smile creases (nasolabial folds) and crow's feet wrinkles reinforce a happy expression beyond just the mouth curve
- Skin tone shading uses three values: base (lightest, e.g. 225,190,155), shadow (mid, e.g. 195,155,120), deep shadow (darkest, e.g. 165,125,95) -- all warm beige-brown family
- Cheek blush (subtle pink hatching on the light side) adds warmth and life to a face
