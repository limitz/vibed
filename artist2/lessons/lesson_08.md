## Concepts

### What Makes a Masterpiece?

A masterpiece is not just a collection of techniques demonstrated side by side. It is a composition where every element serves a unified vision. The techniques disappear into the work -- they become invisible infrastructure supporting something greater than any one of them.

You have learned:
- How to control lines and pressure (the mechanics of mark-making)
- How to draw shapes and curves (the vocabulary of form)
- How to create light and shadow (the illusion of depth and volume)
- How to use color with purpose (emotional temperature and spatial depth)
- How to compose a scene with atmospheric perspective (the illusion of distance)
- How to draw the human form and convey expression (emotional content through representation)
- How to express mood through abstract marks (emotional content freed from representation)

A masterpiece uses all of these -- not by including one example of each, but by weaving them together so tightly that removing any one element would diminish the whole.

## Tips

- **Plan before you code.** Sketch the composition in your mind (or in comments) before writing any drawing calls. Where does the sky end? Where is the figure? Where do abstract marks live? How do the three element types interact?
- **Build in layers.** Start with the most distant, largest elements (sky gradients, distant hills) and work forward to foreground details and abstract overlays. Your landscape and still life lessons taught you this. Apply it.
- **Let the abstract elements grow from the representational ones.** The most powerful compositions are those where you cannot draw a clean line between "the realistic part" and "the abstract part." A sky that becomes increasingly expressionistic as it rises. A foreground that dissolves into pure gesture. Water that is half reflection, half abstract color field.
- **The figure does not need to be large.** A small figure in a vast landscape can be more powerful than a large portrait. Scale communicates meaning -- a tiny human against an enormous sky says something about the relationship between person and world.
- **Use your strongest techniques.** Your oak tree from Lesson 5 was your best single element. Your color shading from Lesson 4 was your best technical skill. Your abstract energy marks from Lesson 7 were your most expressive work. Bring your best, not just your adequate.
