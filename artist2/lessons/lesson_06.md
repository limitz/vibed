## Concepts

### Face Proportions

The human face follows surprisingly consistent proportional rules. These are not exact for every person, but they give you a reliable starting structure:

1. **Head shape**: An upright oval (ellipse taller than wide). The width is roughly 2/3 of the height.
2. **Eye line**: The eyes sit at the **vertical midpoint** of the head -- not near the top as beginners often draw. If the head oval goes from y_top to y_bottom, the eyes are at (y_top + y_bottom) / 2.
3. **Nose**: The bottom of the nose is roughly halfway between the eye line and the chin (bottom of the oval).
4. **Mouth**: The mouth sits roughly 1/3 of the way from the nose to the chin.
5. **Eye spacing**: The distance between the eyes is approximately one eye-width. The head is about 5 eye-widths wide.
6. **Ears**: The tops of the ears align roughly with the eyebrows; the bottoms align roughly with the nose tip.

You do not need to measure these precisely. Use them as guidelines. The goal is a face that looks proportionally reasonable, not a medical diagram.

### Drawing Facial Features

Each facial feature can be built from your existing drawing vocabulary:

- **Eyes**: An ellipse for the eye shape (wide, low). A filled circle inside for the iris/pupil. A curved line above for the eyelid crease. Short lines or curves for eyelashes if desired.
- **Eyebrows**: A curve or S-curve above each eye. Thicker eyebrows (higher pressure, thicker tool) suggest strength or intensity. Thin, arched eyebrows suggest delicacy or surprise.
- **Nose**: A vertical line or gentle curve for the nose bridge. A small arc or curve at the bottom for the nose tip. Two small arcs or dots for nostrils. Keep it simple -- noses that are overdefined look worse than noses that are understated.
- **Mouth**: Two curves -- one for the upper lip (often a flattened M-shape or two connected arcs) and one for the lower lip (a single downward arc). The line between the lips is often the most important mark: it defines whether the mouth is open, closed, smiling, or frowning.
- **Jaw/chin**: The lower portion of the head oval naturally suggests the jaw. You can refine it with a curve if needed.

### Expression Through Line Quality

Expression lives in three places: the **mouth curve**, the **eyebrow angle**, and the **eye shape**. Small changes create big emotional shifts:

| Expression | Mouth | Eyebrows | Eyes | Overall Line Quality |
|-----------|-------|----------|------|---------------------|
| **Happy/smiling** | Upward curve, corners lifted | Slightly raised, gently arched | Open, maybe slightly squinted | Light pressure, flowing curves, open feeling |
| **Sad/melancholy** | Downward curve, corners drooped | Inner ends angled upward (worried brow) | Slightly drooped, maybe half-closed | Heavier pressure, slower curves, weighted feeling |
| **Contemplative/calm** | Neutral, nearly straight, lips together | Level, relaxed | Steady, focused, not wide | Even pressure, controlled lines, stillness |

The tool you choose also affects expression:
- **Pencil** for soft, introspective, quiet portraits
- **Pen** for sharp, defined, intense portraits
- **Charcoal** for rough, emotional, expressive portraits
- **Brush** for soft washes of shading and gentle tone

### Shading the Face

A face is a complex 3D surface, but you can simplify it by thinking of it as a few key shadow zones (assuming light from the upper left):

1. **Light side** (left cheek, left forehead): Minimal shading. Leave mostly open or use very light hatching.
2. **Shadow side** (right cheek, right side of face): Moderate hatching or gradient. The transition from light to shadow happens roughly down the center of the nose bridge.
3. **Under the nose**: A small horizontal shadow where the nose casts onto the upper lip area.
4. **Under the lower lip**: A small shadow beneath the lower lip.
5. **Jaw shadow / under chin**: The area beneath the jaw line is in shadow, giving the face a sense of volume above the neck.
6. **Eye sockets**: Subtle shadows above and around the eyes give them depth instead of looking pasted on.

Use hatching at varied angles and spacings to build these shadows. Crosshatching for the darkest areas (under chin, deep eye shadow), single hatching for medium shadows, and leave the light side open.

### Drawing Hair

Hair is not a solid mass -- it is thousands of strands that flow in directions. You suggest this by:

- Using **many overlapping strokes** that follow the hair's flow direction (curves, S-curves, arcs).
- Varying **pressure** along each stroke: lighter at the edges, heavier in the middle mass.
- Using **different tools** for different textures: charcoal for thick, textured hair; pencil for fine, smooth hair; pen for sharp individual strands at the hairline.
- Leaving some **gaps** in the hair mass to suggest highlights and volume. Solid black hair looks like a helmet.
- Hair has a **parting point or origin** from which the flow radiates outward and downward.

## Tips

- **Start with one face, get it right, then duplicate and modify.** Build the first portrait in full (head, features, shading, hair). Once it works, adapt the same structure for the other two, changing expression-critical elements (mouth curve, eyebrow angle, eye openness).
- **The mouth is the most powerful expression tool.** A smile is just an upward curve. A frown is a downward curve. But the *degree* of curvature, the *length* of the mouth line, and whether the corners turn up or down -- these tiny details control the entire emotional read of the face.
- **Eyebrows are the second most powerful tool.** Angry: inner ends angled sharply downward. Sad: inner ends angled upward (creating a worried inverted-V). Surprised: high arched curves. Calm: level, gentle arcs.
- **Understate the nose.** A nose that is just a short vertical line with a tiny arc at the bottom reads better than a fully modeled nose with nostrils, bridge, and shading. Overdrawn noses are one of the most common beginner mistakes.
- **Skin tone as fill, then hatch on top.** Use `draw_filled_circle` or `draw_filled_rectangle` in a skin tone color as a base, then layer hatching on top for shadows. This gives you the warm base color with shading on top, just like you did with the colored objects in Lesson 4.
- **Hair flows FROM somewhere.** Pick a crown point or parting line and have all hair strokes radiate outward and downward from there. Hair that starts and ends randomly looks like straw.
- **Leave the light side of the face alone.** The temptation is to shade everything. Resist it. The contrast between shaded and unshaded areas is what creates the 3D illusion. If everything is shaded, nothing is.
- **S-curves are your friend for hair.** `draw_s_curve` naturally creates the flowing, organic lines that hair follows. Vary the amplitude for different wave patterns.
